Agentic consulting: A new way of working

Lessons from using AI agents on real consulting projects

After several months of using the latest generation of AI agents in my day-to-day consulting work, I wanted to take a step back and write down what I've actually learned — and share it with a wider audience.

This isn't a survey of the AI market, or a vendor comparison. It's a working consultant's perspective: what I've tried, what's worked, what hasn't, and the mental model I've settled on for getting real value out of these tools on client engagements.

I've come to call this approach agentic consulting.

Agentic consulting is a way of running client engagements in which AI agents operate as a third group of collaborators alongside stakeholders and human consultants — where the consultant's job shifts from producing drafts to orchestrating the flow of context, questions, and judgment across all three.

The core argument of this article is that the most effective way to use AI agents on a consulting project isn't to pick the best tool — it's to redesign how the whole engagement works, treating AI as a third group of intelligence alongside humans, not as a tool that sits next to the humans.

That argument rests on five principles:

  1. No single tool wins. Real consulting work today requires a deliberate stack — each tool used where it's strongest.
  2. Three groups, not two. Modern engagements have three intelligences working together: stakeholders, human consultants, and AI agents. The work succeeds or fails based on how well they hand off to each other.
  3. Human effort moves upstream. Because AI now drafts faster than any human, the high-value human work shifts to asking the right questions and curating the right context.
  4. Best practices exist, and so do limits. A real operating model includes how you run your stack, what tools to match to which deliverable, and an honest look at the connector gap between shipped capability and enterprise reality.
  5. AI wins drafts; humans win judgment. AI is strongest at synthesis, first drafts, and scaffolding. Humans remain irreplaceable at deep domain expertise, pattern recognition, and the credibility to push back on stakeholders.
A note on timing. This reflects my own setup and experience as of mid-2026. Treat this as one practitioner's snapshot, not a definitive map.

A new way of thinking about how we work

The conceptual model I've settled on is that any AI-assisted consulting engagement now has three groups of intelligence working together, and the work succeeds or fails based on how well they hand off to each other. This is the shift — from "humans do the work, tools help" to a genuine three-way collaboration where each group brings something the other two structurally cannot.

Group 1 — Stakeholders

This is client-side leadership plus consulting firm leadership. The people who own the outcome.

  • Role: Set direction and intent.
  • Responsibility: Frame the right strategic questions — the ones that determine whether the work matters at all.
  • Superpower: Industry and business depth. Years of exposure to the client's market, an internal map of the organization, and a feel for which answers will land. None of this lives in a document the AI can read.

Group 2 — Human consultants (the "human MCP")

The traditional consulting workforce — the people in the meetings, on the calls, reading the room.

  • Role: Sense and gather.
  • Responsibility: Capture context for AI. Convert lived experience — client feedback, documents, example files, decisions, project plans — into structured inputs the AI agents can actually use.
  • Superpower: Live access to people and situations, layered with tacit knowledge from past projects. They are, functionally, the project's eyes, ears, and memory.

I find the "human MCP" framing useful. They see, observe, and interact with the project in ways no AI agent can. Their knowledge sits in their brains and is shaped by domain expertise and project history.

Group 3 — AI agents

A deliberate stack of tools (covered in detail in Part 2).

  • Role: Reason and produce.
  • Responsibility: Take the context the first two groups provide and turn it into drafts — analysis, slides, code, models, synthesis.
  • Superpower: Speed, scale, and recall. Instant access to broad knowledge, the ability to draft and revise quickly, and consistency across long or repetitive work.

The structural limitation, which the rest of this article is really about, is that AI agents only ever see a fraction of the project's reality — whatever the first two groups managed to write down and feed in. No amount of context engineering fully closes that gap. Designing for it is the actual job.

What AI actually frees humans up to do

Here's a shift I've noticed over the last several months that I think is underdiscussed.

AI agents now summarize information, draft synthesis, and generate ideas faster than any human can. That's not controversial anymore. But the second-order effect is the interesting part: it changes what the most valuable human work on a project actually is.

If the AI can produce a first draft of almost any analysis in minutes, then drafting is no longer the bottleneck. The bottleneck moves upstream — to the two things only humans can really do well:

  1. Asking the right questions. What is actually worth investigating? What would a senior partner challenge in a steering committee? What's the question the client hasn't articulated yet but actually needs answered?
  2. Curating the right context to feed the system. What does the AI need to see in order to produce a credible answer? What's the minimum, highest-signal information that captures the real shape of the project?

Stakeholders and project managers should be spending a much larger share of their time on these two things — and a much smaller share on producing first drafts. The leverage is in the framing and the feeding, not the typing.

What context is actually worth feeding the AI?

This is the practical question, and it deserves a real answer. In my experience, the highest-value inputs fall into two buckets.

Bucket 1 — Project-defining documents (from the consulting side):

  • The original RFP (Request for Proposal) from the client
  • The NASOW (Negotiated and Agreed Statement of Work)
  • The kickoff deck

These typically contain everything an AI needs to understand the project's frame: objectives, key deliverables, stakeholders, pain points, and the project plan. If you only feed the AI three documents at the start of an engagement, these are the three.

Bucket 2 — Client-side sample data and reference material:

  • Clinical workflow descriptions (or the equivalent process documentation for non-clinical engagements)
  • Data dictionaries
  • Architectural designs

This is where the AI gets a real sense of the client's environment — how their data is structured, how their systems are wired, how work actually flows. Without this, AI output stays generic. With it, you start getting drafts that feel like they were written by someone who understands the account.

Input typeSourceWhat it gives the AI
RFPClient → consulting firmWhy the project exists, what success looks like
NASOWNegotiated, both sidesScope, deliverables, constraints
Kickoff deckConsulting firmStakeholders, plan, early hypotheses
Workflow descriptionsClientHow the business actually operates
Data dictionariesClientWhat the data means, not just what columns exist
Architectural designsClientThe system landscape any solution has to fit into

The pattern I'd encourage: at every project kickoff, treat "what context do we hand to the AI?" as a deliberate work product — not an afterthought. Build a context pack the same way you'd build a stakeholder map. The teams that do this consistently end up multiple steps ahead of teams that just prompt their way through each task in isolation.

The work that still requires deep human expertise

Before closing Part 1, there's a layer worth naming explicitly — because it's easy to read everything above and conclude AI is replacing the senior human work. It's not.

Beyond drafting and synthesis, there is a category of consulting work that genuinely depends on deep domain expertise built up over many years — and this is the work I believe is hardest to replace with AI, probably for a long time.

What I mean by this:

  • The instinct that tells a senior consultant which of the AI's three plausible recommendations is actually the right one for this client, in this industry, at this moment.
  • The pattern recognition that comes from having seen twenty similar transformations and knowing which ones failed and why.
  • The ability to read a client's regulatory, organizational, or cultural context and translate it into what the work actually needs to look like — not what a generic best-practice document says it should look like.
  • The credibility to push back on a stakeholder when the evidence says they're wrong, in a way they'll actually listen to.

None of this is in any document. None of it transfers cleanly into a prompt. AI agents can support this work — pull references, draft options, stress-test arguments — but the expertise itself still lives in the head of a senior practitioner who has spent years earning it.

This isn't a comforting platitude. It's a practical observation about where the leverage points really are. The teams that win with AI won't be the ones that try to automate their seniors out of the loop — they'll be the ones that free their seniors from the drafting work so they have more time to apply the judgment only they can apply.

The stack I actually use

For transparency, the tools currently in rotation:

  • ChatGPT — desktop app, web, and mobile
  • Claude Code — standalone app, CLI, and mobile
  • Codex
  • VS Code as a control room — running Claude, Gemini, and Codex sessions inside it via plugins
  • HTML-file plugins for in-context work
  • Access is a mix of API-based and enterprise-plan-based

No one tool wins across every task. The differences in reasoning depth, coding ability, document handling, IDE integration, mobile continuity, and enterprise data access are large enough that picking one means losing real capability somewhere else.

Best Practices

Five patterns I've learned from running this stack day-to-day. None of these are theoretical — they're the things I wish someone had told me when I started.

1. Use VS Code as your multi-agent control room

The single biggest workflow upgrade I've found is running AI agents inside an environment where I can keep multiple of them visible at once. For me, that's been VS Code — but worth noting upfront that Claude Code's own desktop app has been moving in this direction too, with native support for multiple sessions in a single window.

The pattern I rely on is having multiple agent sessions open in parallel — for example, a Claude session, a Gemini session, and a Codex session, each working a different angle of the same problem. From there you can:

  1. Pass the output of one model to another for a second opinion.
  2. Ask one model to critique and review what another produced.
  3. Effectively have them comment on each other's work.

The handoff today is still mostly manual — you are copy-pasting between sessions — though VS Code's subagent features are starting to automate parts of this, and the community is increasingly building orchestrator-style workflows where one agent delegates to others. The parallelism itself, however you achieve it, is the real unlock.

A second VS Code benefit: everything lives in a local project folder on your laptop. Files, prompts, intermediate artifacts, drafts — all version-controllable, all yours, and not trapped inside a chat history you can't easily export.

Rule of thumb: if a task is high-stakes enough to deserve a second opinion, run it in VS Code with at least two models open.

2. Treat mobile + cloud sessions as a real productivity tool

Both ChatGPT and Claude Code let you continue the same project and session from your phone once your account is connected.

This sounds like a minor convenience but it matters for consulting work specifically:

  • You can keep a long-running project conversation moving while traveling between client sites.
  • You don't need your laptop open for the cloud environment to keep working.
  • Context built up over a week of work is portable — the session doesn't reset because you switched devices.

For consultants who are constantly between meetings, airports, and hotel rooms, this changes when and where the thinking work can happen.

3. Know which limitations come from the tool, and which come from your plan

Usage visibility is wildly inconsistent across the stack — and it matters more than people admit, because it directly affects whether you feel comfortable running an exploratory session at all.

SetupUsage visibilityWhat it feels like in practice
Enterprise plan (VS Code, API-backed)None — no admin dashboard exposed to the end userYou hit a "limit reached" message with no warning and no idea what the quota was
Standalone Claude Code appFull — clear view of quota and consumptionReassuring, but you watch the cost meter tick and second-guess every session
VS Code CLI (Codex / Claude Code)Middle ground — per-session cost visible on demandUseful, but cost is right in your face whenever you check

There's a real product opportunity here: somewhere between "no visibility" and "every token visible" is a saner experience for a working professional. Right now you're either flying blind or watching the meter.

4. Match the deliverable to the model's strengths

Not every model is equally good at every output format. After enough trial and error, here's the rough mapping I use:

DeliverableBest pathNotes
HTML one-pagers, dashboards, interactive prototypesAny major modelConsistently strong across the stack — the web is well-represented in training data
PowerPoint (.pptx)Claude Code (app or VS Code CLI)Still genuinely hard for most models; Claude Code is the most usable today
Code & technical artifactsClaude Code or Codex in VS CodeIDE integration and local file access matter more than raw model quality here
Long-form written analysisChatGPT or Claude (web/desktop)Easier to iterate on long-form text in a chat UI than in an IDE pane
Live internet research & market scansGemini (primary), Claude (secondary)Gemini's native Google Search integration gives it an edge on freshness; Claude is stronger when synthesis matters more than breadth

Practical takeaway: if the client doesn't care about the format, default to HTML. If they need slides, don't force your default model through it — switch to whichever tool is strongest at that format.

5. The connector gap is the real ceiling on enterprise AI

If there's one place where the enterprise AI experience still lags meaningfully behind what's possible, it's MCP connectors — the integrations that let AI agents read from and act on the tools you actually work in.

An honest caveat first: many of the connectors I'd want do now exist as products. Microsoft 365 connectors for Claude (covering Outlook, SharePoint, OneDrive, and Teams) shipped during 2026. The bottleneck for me isn't whether the technology exists — it's whether my specific enterprise environment has enabled them. In practice, I'm still typically limited to a narrow set: enterprise GitHub and Atlassian tools (Jira, Confluence). Other connectors, including Figma, remain unavailable in my setup.

This is an important distinction. The story isn't "the tools can't do this yet." The story is "the tools can, but enterprise policy, security review, and rollout timelines often haven't caught up." Which is its own kind of friction.

The version of full connector access I'd want to actually use day-to-day:

Code & tickets✅ GitHub, Jira, ConfluenceSame — already solid
Daily communication❌ (M365 connectors exist but not enabled for me)Microsoft Teams, Outlook
Document source of truth❌ (same situation)SharePoint

When that surface area opens up — whether because vendors ship more connectors or because enterprise IT enables the ones that already exist — AI shifts from being a smart assistant next to my work to a participant inside my work. The difference is enormous.

Until then, a lot of the value of AI on consulting projects is left on the table — not because the models can't do the work, and increasingly not even because the connectors don't exist, but because the connectors haven't yet reached the day-to-day enterprise user.

Where this has actually worked: real use cases

It's easy to talk about AI in the abstract. Easier still to be skeptical. So here are the concrete use cases where I've seen this stack deliver real value on consulting projects — work that genuinely changed my output, not just demos.

1. Synthesis and discovery

These are the tasks where AI's ability to read fast and summarize structurally is most obvious.

  • Summarizing project documentation for use case discovery and prioritization. Feed in the RFP, kickoff materials, and any reference docs, and the AI can surface candidate use cases, group them by theme, and propose a first-pass prioritization. What used to take a junior consultant a full day takes a focused hour.
  • Quick technology assessment and comparison. Give the AI a shortlist of tools, vendors, or platforms and a set of evaluation criteria — and you get back a credible first draft of a comparison matrix that a human can sharpen rather than build from scratch.
  • Fast, AI-assisted internet research. Instead of opening twenty browser tabs, you can ask an AI agent to research a market, a competitor, a regulation, or an emerging technology — and get back a synthesized answer with sources. Worth calling out the tool difference here: Gemini tends to perform best on this specific task because it's natively connected to Google Search. Claude does a strong job too, especially when you want the search results synthesized into structured, well-reasoned writing.

2. Communication and reporting

The cadence work that consumes more hours than anyone wants to admit.

  • Weekly and monthly project status updates. Feed in the prior week's notes, decisions, and progress, and the AI drafts a clean status update in your project's standard format.
  • Project deliverable drafting and progress showcases. First drafts of the actual deliverables — analyses, write-ups, stakeholder-facing summaries — produced quickly enough that you can iterate three times before the meeting instead of once.

3. Planning and structure

Where AI helps you put scaffolding in place fast.

  • Project plans with timelines. Given the scope, deliverables, and key milestones, the AI drafts a credible project plan — workstreams, dependencies, and a timeline you can refine in your tool of choice.

4. Building and prototyping

This is where I've been most surprised at how far the tools have come.

  • Mockups for web and mobile applications. Going from a verbal description or a rough wireframe to a clickable HTML or React mockup in a single working session — something I can show to a stakeholder in the same meeting where the idea came up. This collapses a feedback loop that used to take a week.
  • Analytics pipelines. Building the scaffolding for a data pipeline — schema, transformations, validation logic — with the AI doing the first 70% of the work and a human handling the integration and edge cases.

5. Client engagement and facilitation

This one is a step beyond the others — and the use case I'm most excited about, because it changes what's possible in a room with a client.

  • Lab design — building interactive workshop experiences. When we run client labs around critical strategic questions (use case prioritization being the most common), the traditional setup is sticky notes, slides, and whiteboards. With AI-assisted web coding, I can now build a custom interactive web app for the workshop itself — a live prioritization tool, a scoring matrix participants can manipulate in real time, a simulator that shows how their choices play out. The experience becomes hands-on rather than passive, and the artifacts that come out of the lab are usable digital outputs instead of photographs of flip-chart paper. This is the kind of differentiation that used to require a dedicated development team and a six-week timeline. With AI, it's now a one- to two-day build by the same consultants running the lab.

What the pattern tells you

Look at the list and you can see where AI is actually strongest today:

Type of workWhy AI is good at it

Those are still firmly in stakeholder and human-consultant territory — and probably will be for a while.

The pattern: AI is winning at the work that produces drafts and structure. Humans are still winning at the work that produces judgment and trust. A good engagement uses both deliberately.

The takeaway

The most effective way to use the latest AI agents on a consulting project is not to pick the "best" tool. It's to:

  1. Run a deliberate stack of complementary tools, each used where it's strongest.
  2. Treat AI as the third group, not the only group — and recognize that stakeholders and consultants hold information AI structurally cannot access.
  3. Move human effort upstream. Spend less time drafting, more time asking the right questions and curating the right context for the AI to work from.
  4. Engineer the handoffs. Most of the value today comes from how well human context flows into the AI, and how well AI output flows back for review.
  5. Push for connector access at the enterprise level. The next 10x in productivity won't come from a smarter model — it'll come from giving the model the right doors into Teams, SharePoint, Outlook, Figma, and the OS itself.

The teams that get this right won't be the ones with the fanciest single tool. They'll be the ones that built the cleanest pipeline between the three groups — and who fought hardest to expand what AI is actually allowed to touch.

That's what I mean by agentic consulting: not consulting with AI, but consulting built around a deliberate three-way collaboration between stakeholders, human consultants, and AI agents. The tools will keep changing. The principle of how to organize the work around them, I think, will not.

A closing note on what I haven't tried yet

In the spirit of honesty about where this article is a snapshot rather than a map: there are several patterns the broader community has been adopting that I personally haven't had a chance to set up or explore in depth yet:

  • Agent teams / multi-agent orchestration — coordinated teams of AI agents working on a shared codebase or task with automatic handoffs, not just parallel sessions I copy-paste between.
  • Scheduled / cloud-triggered AI workflows (sometimes called "routines") — AI tasks that run on a schedule or in response to a GitHub event, even when my laptop is off.
  • Git worktrees for isolated parallel agents — letting multiple agents work on separate feature branches of the same repo without stepping on each other.
  • The M365 connector for Claude in environments where it's enabled — I've described what I'd want from it, but I haven't yet worked with it in a real engagement.
  • Dedicated multi-agent desktop tools like CodeAgentSwarm or the newer Claude Code desktop redesign — I've been getting by with VS Code and standalone apps; these may be a better fit.

This part is about a different question: once one team gets agentic consulting working, how do you help every other team adopt it without each one having to re-discover the same answers?

Because that's what I'm seeing happen in practice. One team experiments, struggles for a few weeks, eventually figures out a workflow that works. Then a second team starts from scratch and goes through the same struggle. Then a third. Each team is paying the same tuition. The firm pays it many times over.

That's the scaling problem. And solving it is, I'd argue, the highest-leverage AI investment a consulting organization can make right now — higher than any individual tool license.

The questions every team re-discovers

When a team starts using AI agents seriously on client work, they hit the same questions. I've watched colleagues work through this list, and I've worked through it myself. The recurring ones, grouped by category:

Tooling and setup

  1. Which tool do I use? ChatGPT, Claude Code, Codex, Gemini, VS Code with plugins — for this specific task, on this specific engagement, which one is right?
  2. How do I set up a shared project environment? Where do we put the prompts, the context files, the intermediate outputs, so the team can collaborate without stepping on each other?

Operational limits

  1. What do we do when we reach the file limit? Models have context window ceilings; enterprise plans have usage ceilings. How do you keep working when you hit them?
  2. How do we compact the memory? Long-running engagement conversations get heavy. How do you preserve the important context while shedding the noise?

Compliance

  1. How do we process client confidential data to navigate legal and compliance requirements? Which tools are approved for which data classifications? What's the safe path for working with PHI, PII, or sensitive commercial data?

Output quality

  1. How do we best generate a report in the format preferred by our firm or the client? PowerPoint templates, document conventions, branding requirements — how do we get AI output that lands in our format, not generic AI format?

Scaling-specific (the two questions teams only hit once they look up from their own engagement)

  1. How do we share what works across teams? When one team figures out a great workflow, how does the next team benefit without sitting through a one-hour explanation?
  2. Who owns this at the firm level? Without clear ownership, every answer above stays informal — and stays slow.

Every team navigating these questions alone is a small, expensive failure of organizational learning. The right response isn't to write a memo telling people the answers. It's to build shared infrastructure so most of these questions are answered before a team encounters them.

A tiered enablement model for scaling

What works, in my experience, is thinking about scaling in three layers. Each layer reduces what the next team has to figure out from scratch.

Layer 1 — A shared knowledge base

The minimum viable version. A single, curated, searchable place where any consultant can find:

  • A tool decision guide — for tasks A, B, C, use tool X with these settings.
  • A starter context pack — the standard prompts, templates, and "system messages" that worked across multiple engagements.
  • Compliance guardrails — clear documentation of which tools are approved for which data classifications, with worked examples.
  • Format templates — branded report skeletons, slide templates, and "house style" prompts that produce output in the firm's voice and shape.
  • A live "what works" log — short writeups from teams who tried something useful, with concrete prompts and outcomes.

This layer is mostly a writing problem, not a building problem. It costs the firm a few dedicated people-weeks per quarter to maintain — far less than the cost of letting every team rediscover the basics. The trap to avoid: a static wiki page that's six months out of date. This needs an owner.

Layer 2 — Shared environments and reusable assets

The next level up: don't just describe what works, ship it.

  • Pre-configured project environments — a VS Code workspace with the right plugins, the firm's CLAUDE.md conventions, the standard folder structure, and the compliance configuration already in place. A new team clones it on day one instead of building it from scratch.
  • Reusable prompt libraries — version-controlled, tested prompts for the common use cases from Part 2 (status updates, tech comparisons, RFP synthesis, lab design scaffolds). Improvements flow back to the library.
  • A shared context-pack template — the structure for the "context as a deliberate work product" idea from Part 1, ready to fill in at every kickoff. RFP, NASOW, kickoff deck, data dictionaries — slots labeled and ready.
  • Approved tool sandboxes — environments where teams can experiment with new tools and data scenarios without having to clear each experiment through full compliance review each time.

This is where the firm's investment starts compounding. Every team that uses the shared environment makes it slightly better for the next team.

Layer 3 — A dedicated enablement function

The level most firms haven't reached yet. A small, dedicated team — sometimes called an "AI Center of Excellence" though I prefer the more grounded enablement team — whose job is specifically to help engagement teams adopt agentic consulting fast.

What they actually do:

  • Office hours for engagement teams hitting blockers.
  • Embedded support on high-stakes engagements where the team is new to this way of working.
  • Active curation of the knowledge base in Layer 1 and the assets in Layer 2 — the assets don't maintain themselves.
  • Pattern propagation — when one team invents something useful (a great workflow, a new use case like lab design), the enablement team's job is to package it so other teams can pick it up in days, not months.
  • Procurement and compliance liaison — owning the relationship with the firm's IT, security, and legal teams so individual engagement teams don't each have to renegotiate access.

This function doesn't need to be large. In my experience a handful of dedicated people can support a practice of hundreds, because their job is leverage — they multiply other teams' effectiveness, not produce client deliverables directly.

A note for leaders

If you're a partner or director reading this and wondering where to invest: my honest view is that the bottleneck on AI value in consulting today is not the tools, and not even individual consultant skill. The bottleneck is shared infrastructure that lets every team start from a paved path instead of bushwhacking.

Buying more enterprise AI seats won't fix this. Mandating AI training won't fix this. The thing that fixes this is a small enablement team with clear ownership, a curated knowledge base, and the authority to ship shared environments and templates that every engagement team can pick up on day one.

The ROI math is straightforward. If a typical engagement team currently spends 40-80 hours over the first month figuring out the questions above, and there are dozens of engagement teams running concurrently, the firm is paying for those hours every month, forever, until the infrastructure exists. The cost of building the infrastructure is roughly one team's worth of dedicated effort. The savings repeat every month after that.

Closing the loop

Part 1 made the case that an agentic consulting engagement has three groups of intelligence: stakeholders, human consultants, and AI agents.

Part 2 walked through how one team can run that model effectively.

Part 3 adds a fourth group — operating at the firm level, not the engagement level:

The enablement layer. The shared people, knowledge, and infrastructure whose job is to ensure that what one team learns benefits every other team — fast.

Without this fourth group, agentic consulting stays a story of individual heroics. With it, it becomes a capability the firm actually owns.

Agentic consulting isn't a tool. It isn't a methodology. It's a way of organizing work — and at firm scale, organizing how learning about how to work spreads. The teams that figure this out will move several times faster than the teams that don't.

This is Part 3 of a series on agentic consulting. If you're leading or scaling AI adoption in a consulting context, I'd love to compare notes. What's worked for spreading what one team learns to the rest of your practice — and what hasn't?
